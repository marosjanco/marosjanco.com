This is a narrow post about a problem most people never hit, written for the few who will hit it hard: anyone building AI tooling over Slovak or Czech law. The short version is that a legal citation from before 1993 can legitimately belong to two legal lineages at once, and a system that does not resolve that ambiguity deterministically will be confidently wrong in a way users cannot see.

## The setup

Slovakia and the Czech Republic share a Czechoslovak past. After the federation split, both successor states carried forward parts of the federal legal corpus and then diverged. The practical consequence for tooling is that a citation written in a pre-split format does not, on its own, tell you which lineage it belongs to. The same act-number-and-year shape can resolve to a federal act, or to the Slovak continuation, or to the Czech continuation, depending on context the citation string does not contain.

A lawyer reading the surrounding text disambiguates this without thinking about it, because they hold the context. A retrieval system, and a language model, do not — unless you give them a deterministic way to.

## Why this breaks naive AI legal tools

The naive pipeline is: user asks a question, retrieve relevant passages, let the model answer with citations. Every stage of that quietly mishandles the ambiguity:

- **Retrieval** keyed on the citation string conflates documents from different lineages that share a citation shape, so the context window gets a mix and the model has no signal about which is authoritative for the question.
- **The model**, asked to produce a citation, will emit a plausible one. Faced with an ambiguous reference it does not surface the ambiguity — it picks, confidently, and the wrong pick is indistinguishable from the right one in the output.
- **The user** sees a clean citation to a real provision and has no way to know it resolved down the wrong lineage. As with hallucinated source URLs, the dangerous failure is not the broken citation — it is the plausible, wrong one.

The deeper issue is the same one that shows up across production LLM work: an ambiguity that has a deterministic resolution rule is being resolved probabilistically, where the cost of a silent wrong answer is high.

## The fix: resolve it in code, fail loudly

Lexomat does not let the model resolve the lineage. The model identifies the citation; a resolution layer maps it to a concrete document. That layer:

1. **Parses the citation into structured components** rather than treating it as a string — act number, year, version, paragraph, and any lineage markers actually present.
2. **Applies explicit disambiguation rules.** Where a deterministic rule exists (the reference carries enough signal, or the surrounding query constrains the jurisdiction), it resolves to exactly one document and a verified source link.
3. **Fails loudly when the reference is genuinely ambiguous.** This is the important part. If the citation cannot be resolved to a single lineage with confidence, the system says so rather than silently choosing. A surfaced "this reference is ambiguous between X and Y" is a correct, useful answer in a legal product. A confident wrong pick is not.

This is the same architectural stance as not letting the model construct its own URLs: anything with a deterministic correct form is produced by code from structured model output, and the system is allowed — required — to refuse.

## Why "just add more context" is not the answer

The tempting shortcut is to stuff lineage hints into the prompt and few-shot the model through the ambiguous cases. It moves the error rate; it does not remove the error class. The ambiguity is rule-shaped, the rules are knowable, and the cost of a silent miss is a wrong legal citation in front of a professional. That combination is exactly when you take the decision out of the probabilistic component and put it in tested code with explicit cases — including a deliberate test suite of known-ambiguous references that must resolve to "ambiguous", not to a guess.

## Why this is worth a post

Most of this work is invisible when it succeeds: the citations are simply right, and the ambiguous ones are flagged instead of fabricated. It is also the kind of domain detail that does not generalise — and that is the point. Production AI in a specialised domain is mostly a stack of these. The model is the easy, generic part. The defensible product is the accumulated set of small, deterministic, domain-correct decisions around it that a generic system gets quietly wrong. Slovak/Czechoslovak citation disambiguation is one of mine; every serious vertical AI product has its own list.
